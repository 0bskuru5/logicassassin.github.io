The National Museum of Ethiopia

Description

The National Museum of Ethiopia stands out as one of the most remarkable museums in sub-Saharan Africa, boasting an impressive collection of priceless artifacts. Among its treasures is the world-famous skeleton of Lucy, a 3.2 million-year-old human ancestor. The museum is organized into four primary sections, each focusing on a different aspect of Ethiopia's rich history and culture. The basement is devoted to paleontological discoveries, including extinct creatures and early human fossils. On the ground floor, visitors can explore exhibits from the pre-Aksumite, Aksumite, Solomonic, and Gonderian periods, which feature ancient royal paraphernalia, Sabaean inscriptions, and other fascinating artifacts, such as a 4th-century-BC rock-hewn chair adorned with mythical ibexes and an elaborate bronze oil lamp dating back to before the 1st century AD. The first floor showcases Ethiopian art, from 14th-century parchment to 20th-century oil paintings, including the monumental African Heritage painting by Afewerk Tekle, which depicts the legendary meeting of King Solomon and the Queen of Sheba. The second floor houses a diverse collection of secular arts and crafts, such as traditional weapons, jewelry, utensils, clothing, and musical instruments.
